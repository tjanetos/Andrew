About Me

I am a third year graduate student at Northwestern University where I am pursuing a Ph.D. in personality, development, and health as well as a master's degree in statistics. I graduated from Duke University in spring 2015 with a B.S. in psychology and a minor in statistical science.

Broadly, I study how personality processes impact health outcomes over the life course. Specifically, I am interested in the ways in which facets of the personality trait of conscientiousness (e.g., self-control) and neuroticism develop over time to impact key health outcomes such as medication adherence and cardiovascular disease risk.

Toward these aims, I implement growth curve modeling, psychometrics, and machine learning in my work, focusing on intra-individual trajectories of personality development over time. I have key interests in open science, namely, how we can improve common scientific practices to enhance the replicability of psychological science. In this vein, my lab utilizes the IALSA (Integrative Analysis of Longitudinal Studies on Aging) framework to conduct integrative data analyses (IDAs) in which we investigate the replicability of personality development trajectories over time across many longitudinal samples.
